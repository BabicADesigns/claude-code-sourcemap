# Storytelling

A practical framework for making narrative writing of any length pull a reader or
viewer through to the end, instead of letting their attention drift. It distills the
techniques short-form creator Kane Kallaway (1B+ views across his videos) teaches in
*"How To Become A Master Storyteller"* — useful for video/podcast scripts as he
intended, but the same mechanics apply to blog posts, product narratives, pitches,
and any other writing that needs to hold attention rather than just convey facts.

## When to use this skill

Use it whenever a piece of writing needs to be **engaging**, not just correct:
scripts, intros/hooks, brand or product stories, blog posts, talks, pitches, social
copy, narrative passages inside otherwise technical docs. Use it both to draft new
material and to punch up an existing draft that reads as flat, listy, or boring.

Don't force it onto writing that should stay plain and reference-like — API docs,
changelogs, legal text, or anything where the reader wants to scan for facts, not be
pulled through a narrative. Applying hooks and rhythm there adds noise, not value.

## The core idea: stories are a dance, not a list

Weak writing strings facts together with **"and then"**: this happened, and then
this happened, and then this happened. Each beat just sits next to the last one —
nothing connects them, so a reader's brain has no reason to keep paying attention.
It can stop at any point and lose nothing.

Strong writing alternates two kinds of beats:

- **Context ("therefore")** — stable ground. Who/what/where, the consequence of the
  last beat, the plan. This is what closes the open question the last beat raised.
- **Conflict ("but")** — something disrupts the stable ground: a problem, a twist,
  a contradiction, a cost. This is what opens a new question in the reader's head.

A story is therefore a repeating dance: **context → but (conflict) → therefore
(resolution/consequence) → but (new conflict) → ...** Conflict is what makes a
reader's brain itch to find out what happens next; context is what lets that itch
get scratched just enough to set up the next one. A piece built entirely of context
is dull (nothing's at stake); a piece built entirely of conflict is exhausting
(nothing ever lands). The alternation is what creates pull.

**Rewrite test:** read your draft's beats back as a list and check the connective
tissue between each one.
- *Weak:* "We shipped the feature. **And then** support tickets started piling
  up. **And then** we found the root cause."
- *Strong:* "We shipped the feature, **but** support tickets started piling up
  within a day. **Therefore** we pulled the team off the roadmap to dig into why —
  **but** the root cause turned out to be somewhere nobody had looked."

If you can swap "and then" back in without losing meaning, the beat isn't doing
any work — either cut it or give it a real "but"/"therefore" relationship to its
neighbor.

## Five more techniques to layer on top

1. **Break predictable patterns.** Brains are pattern-recognition machines: once a
   reader detects the shape of your sentences or section structure, that part of
   their brain switches off and drifts elsewhere, even if the content is still
   good. If you've written three sentences in a row with the same length and
   shape, or three sections with an identical setup, deliberately break the
   pattern on the next one.

2. **Give it rhythm.** Vary sentence length on purpose — short, punchy sentences
   next to longer, flowing ones. A paragraph of uniformly medium sentences reads
   as monotone even if every sentence is well-written. Read a draft out loud (or
   simulate it) and listen for whether it has a beat, not just correct grammar.

3. **Hook more than once.** Don't treat "the hook" as something that only lives in
   the first line. Structure the whole piece as a repeating cycle of
   setup-then-rehook: every section should end on an open question, a stake, or a
   reason to keep going, not a fully resolved thought. If a reader could stop
   after any paragraph and feel like they got the full picture, you've under-hooked
   the middle.

4. **Find a unique angle on a familiar topic.** If the subject has been covered a
   hundred times before, generic treatment makes the piece forgettable no matter
   how well-structured it is. Look for the specific, surprising, or counterintuitive
   lens — a concrete detail, an unexpected comparison, a contrarian take — that this
   particular piece can own.

5. **Write like you talk.** Default technical or marketing phrasing is stiffer than
   how the writer would actually explain the thing to a friend. Favor direct,
   conversational phrasing over formal hedging — it closes the distance between
   writer and reader and makes the piece feel like it's actually being told to
   someone, not published at them.

## Process: write the bookends first

When drafting from scratch, write the opening hook and the closing line before
filling in the middle. Decide where the piece needs to land emotionally or
practically, and what's going to grab attention at the very top, before building
the bridge between them. Writing the middle first tends to produce meandering
beats with no destination, because nothing is pulling them toward an ending yet.

## Self-check before delivering a draft

Run a finished draft against this list and fix what fails:

- [ ] Read the beats back-to-back — is any pair connected only by "and then"? If
      so, give it a real "but" (conflict) or "therefore" (consequence) instead.
- [ ] Could a reader stop after the first paragraph/section and feel satisfied? If
      yes, the hook isn't being re-planted through the middle — add an open
      question or stake partway through.
- [ ] Are three or more consecutive sentences the same approximate length/shape?
      Break it up.
- [ ] Does this read like a press release about the topic, or does it have a
      specific angle nobody else would have used for this exact piece?
- [ ] Would the writer actually say this out loud to a person, or does it sound
      like it was written to satisfy a template?

This checklist is a diagnostic, not a formula — apply judgment about which beats
genuinely need rework versus which are fine as plain context (not every sentence
needs conflict; pieces need places to breathe between the pulls).
