# BabicADesigns Brand System

**Product:** Balkanish Planner
**Tagline:** The Balkanish AI Way
**Version:** 1.0 — Brand Layer Phase 1

This document is the source of truth for how BabicADesigns sounds, looks, and behaves in writing. If a sentence on the site couldn't have been written by a well-traveled Croatian friend with great taste — rewrite it.

---

## 1. Mission

Most travel platforms sell you a destination. We introduce you to one.

Balkanish Planner exists because the Balkans keep getting flattened into a stop on someone else's itinerary — a cheaper Croatia, a quieter Greece, a "hidden gem" that's been on six listicles this year. We built this for the traveler who wants the version a local would actually recommend: the konoba with no sign, the swim spot with no name on Google Maps, the rule about coffee that nobody writes down because everyone already knows it.

We are not building a database of attractions. We're building the closest thing to a local friend that software can be.

## 2. Positioning

**What we are:** An editorial, AI-assisted guide to the Balkans written and curated like a magazine, not generated like a brochure.

**What we are not:**
- A booking engine (we don't sell rooms, flights, or tours)
- A listicle factory ("Top 10 Things to Do in Dubrovnik")
- A tourism board (we're allowed to say a place is overrated)
- A generic AI wrapper (every itinerary is shaped by point of view, not just preferences)

**Category we're really in:** Travel media with a planning tool attached, not a planning tool with travel content attached. The personality comes first; the AI Planner is one of the things the personality produces, not the product the personality decorates.

**Positioning line (internal use):** "The opinionated friend who happens to know everything, not the algorithm that happens to be friendly."

## 3. Audience

People who already know how to use a map app. They don't need directions — they need judgment. They are mid-trip-planning when they find us, slightly fatigued by content that all reads the same, and looking for a second opinion they can trust. They've usually traveled enough to recognize when something is written by someone who has actually been there.

## 4. Brand Personality

Five traits, in order of how often they should show up:

1. **Warm** — like being looked after, not marketed to.
2. **Intelligent** — informed opinions, specific knowledge, no hand-waving.
3. **Soulful** — places matter because of what they mean, not just what they look like.
4. **Slightly humorous** — dry, observational, never a pun for its own sake.
5. **Culturally curious & anti-tourist** — interested in why a place is the way it is, allergic to "must-see" thinking.

**The throughline:** anti-hustle. Nothing on this site should ever feel like it's trying to maximize your itinerary. The Balkans rewards slowing down; the copy should model that, not contradict it with urgency.

### Voice in one sentence
**A well-traveled Croatian friend with great taste, telling you the truth about a place because they respect you too much to sell it to you.**

### We must never sound like:
- A **tourism board** ("A land of contrasts, waiting to be discovered")
- A **travel agency** ("Book now and save!")
- **SEO content** ("Top 15 Best Things To Do In Split In 2025")
- A **corporate AI** ("I'd be happy to help you plan your trip!")

If a sentence could appear verbatim on a destination-marketing website, it's wrong for this brand. If a sentence sounds like it came from a chatbot apologizing or offering to help, it's wrong for this brand.

## 5. Tone of Voice — the rules

1. **Specific beats superlative.** Never "the most beautiful beach in Croatia." Always the one detail that proves it — the smell, the sound, the thing the owner said.
2. **Show the trade-off.** Every real place has one. Name it. ("No ATM, no Wi-Fi, no regrets" tells the truth instead of hiding it.)
3. **Earn the praise.** Don't open with how good something is — open with why, and let the reader arrive at "this sounds good" themselves.
4. **Short sentences carry weight. Use them after a longer one.** Rhythm matters more than rules of grammar.
5. **Humor is dry, not jokey.** It should read like a raised eyebrow, not a punchline. If you'd need a laugh-track, cut it.
6. **Second person, friend register.** "You" not "travelers." Talk to one person sitting across the table, not a market segment.
7. **Confidence without arrogance.** State opinions plainly ("Skip the funicular, it's a tourist queue with a view") without over-explaining or hedging into mush.
8. **Never apologize for having an opinion.** No "in our opinion," no "we think," no "personally." Just say the thing.
9. **Let imperfection be charming.** Ferry delays, grumpy konoba owners, a town that closes for three hours at lunch — these are texture, not flaws to smooth over.

## 6. Writing Rules — mechanics

- **Sentence length:** Vary it on purpose. A 20-word setup followed by a 4-word payoff is the house rhythm.
- **Adjectives:** One precise adjective beats three vague ones. Cut "amazing," "stunning," "breathtaking," "must-see" on sight — see banned words below.
- **Numbers and stats:** Use them sparingly and only when they tell a story (e.g., "closed to outsiders for 44 years," not "rated 4.8 stars").
- **Em dashes and colons:** Allowed, used for a turn of thought — not as a tic. Don't stack more than one per sentence.
- **Contractions:** Always use them. "Don't," "it's," "you'll." Formal copy reads corporate.
- **Local words:** Use real Balkan vocabulary (konoba, rakija, pomalo, fjaka) in italics on first use per page with a quick, unpatronizing gloss. Never explain a word the way a textbook would — explain it the way a friend would, in passing.
- **Paragraphs:** Short. Two to four sentences in body copy. White space is part of the voice — it signals unhurried, not unfinished.
- **Headlines:** Should work as a stand-alone sentence, not a label. "Travel the Balkans like someone invited you" is a headline. "Balkans Travel Guide" is a label. Always choose the sentence.

## 7. CTA Style

CTAs should sound like an invitation, never a transaction.

**Good:**
- "Plan My Trip with AI"
- "Find My Swap"
- "Start Slow"
- "Get the Local Read"
- "See What Locals Skip"

**Bad:**
- "Sign Up Now"
- "Get Started Today!"
- "Click Here"
- "Unlock Your Itinerary"
- "Don't Miss Out"

Rules:
- No urgency words (now, today, limited, don't miss).
- No exclamation points. Confidence doesn't need volume.
- Verb-first, specific to the action, never generic ("Learn More," "Submit").
- Premium/paid CTAs should sound like access, not a sale: "Get the Full Guide," not "Buy Now."

## 8. Content Principles

1. **Every place gets a reason, not just a description.** If we can't say *why* a place matters beyond how it looks, it doesn't get a feature slot.
2. **Always name the trade-off.** Crowds, distance, awkward hours, an outhouse instead of a bathroom — say it. Trust is the product.
3. **One human detail per piece, minimum.** A name, an overheard line, a specific dish at a specific table. Generic copy has zero; ours should never have zero.
4. **Anti-hustle by default.** No countdowns, no "before it's too late," no manufactured scarcity. The Balkans aren't going anywhere; neither is the reader's right to take their time.
5. **Opinions are content.** "Skip this," "this is overrated," "locals actually go here instead" are more valuable than another adjective-laden description.
6. **Humor punches up at tourism culture, never down at the places or people.** We can joke about cruise-ship crowds. We do not joke about the Balkans or Balkan people.
7. **AI is a tool in the voice's hand, not a replacement for it.** Anything AI-generated (itineraries, suggestions) should still read like it passed through the same editorial filter as hand-written copy — specific, opinionated, warm.

## 9. Examples — Good vs. Bad Copy

| Situation | ❌ Generic / Bad | ✅ Balkanish / Good |
|---|---|---|
| Hero headline | "Discover the Beauty of the Balkans" | "Travel the Balkans like someone invited you." |
| Destination intro | "Vis is a beautiful island with crystal-clear water and a rich history." | "Closed to foreign visitors for decades as a military base, Vis missed the package-tour wave entirely. What's left is a slow island of stone villages, vineyards, and a harbour that still smells like fish nets, not sunscreen." |
| CTA button | "Get Started Now!" | "Plan My Trip with AI" |
| Food copy | "Burek is a delicious pastry enjoyed throughout the region." | "Burek is breakfast, an argument, and a hangover cure, depending who you ask and what time it is." |
| Crowd warning | "This popular destination can get busy during peak season." | "If you can see the bell tower in every photo, you're not early enough." |
| Empty state | "No results found. Try adjusting your filters." | "Nothing matches yet — pomalo, try loosening a filter." |
| AI Planner intro | "Our AI will generate a personalized itinerary based on your preferences." | "Tell us your dates, budget, and travel style. We'll build the day-by-day like a friend who's already done the trip — gaps for getting lost included." |
| Sign-up prompt | "Create an account to save your favorites." | "Keep a running list of the places you'd actually go back to." |

## 10. Brand Vocabulary

Words and phrases that belong to this brand — use deliberately, not as decoration:

- **Pomalo** — "slowly, slowly" / "no rush." The closest thing the Balkans has to a national philosophy.
- **Konoba** — a small, usually family-run tavern. Not a "restaurant."
- **Fjaka** — the specific, almost sacred Dalmatian state of doing absolutely nothing.
- **Rakija** — homemade fruit brandy; also a verb for hospitality ("they rakija'd us within five minutes").
- **Auntie-knows** — the unverifiable but always-correct wisdom passed down at a family table.
- **The Balkanish Way** — our shorthand for doing things the slower, more local, more honest way.
- **Local-first** — locals' opinions outrank algorithms, reviews, and "trending" lists.
- **Invitation, not itinerary** — the feeling we're going for: this was offered to you, not sold to you.

## 11. Banned Words & Phrases

Cut on sight, no exceptions:

- "Amazing," "stunning," "breathtaking," "magical," "unforgettable," "epic"
- "Must-see," "must-visit," "bucket list," "hidden gem" (ironic, since that's a section name — inside that section, never use the literal phrase to describe a place; show it, don't label it)
- "Paradise," "exotic," "untouched," "unspoiled," "off the beaten path"
- "Discover," "unlock," "explore" as empty verbs ("Discover the magic of...")
- "Top [number]," "Best of," "Ultimate Guide"
- "In today's fast-paced world," "in this day and age"
- "Don't miss out," "limited time," "act now," "hurry"
- "I'd be happy to," "as an AI," "I hope this helps," "feel free to" — any chatbot-register filler
- "Personalized experience," "seamless," "elevate your journey," "curated for you" — marketing-speak with no specific referent
- Exclamation points in CTAs or headlines (body copy, very rare exceptions only, and never more than one per page)

## 12. Voice Checklist (use before publishing any copy)

- [ ] Could this sentence appear on a tourism board's website? If yes, rewrite it.
- [ ] Does it name a real trade-off, not just a real upside?
- [ ] Is there one specific, human, unverifiable-but-true detail?
- [ ] Would a well-traveled Croatian friend actually say this out loud?
- [ ] Did I cut every word from the banned list?
- [ ] Is the CTA an invitation, not a transaction?
